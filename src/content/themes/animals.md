Animal groups have a remarkable capacity to collectively navigate, forage, hunt, and avoid predators. My early research focused on mechanisms of effective collective behavior in fish schools, including how fish orient to currents (rheotaxis) alone and in groups — combining touch, vision, vestibular cues, and the movements of others.

A [2020 review](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/223/23/jeb223008/226046/Rheotaxis-revisited-a-multi-behavioral-and) synthesizes that line of work. Related papers address group living benefits from fish to zebras, predator–prey dynamics in schools, and vortex phase matching in schooling robots and fish.
